Neural Kinematic Bases for Fluids

Yibo Liu1, Zhixin Fang2, Sune Darkner3, Noam Aigerman4, Kenny Erleben3, Paul Kry5, Teseo Schneider1
1 University of Victoria, 2 Inworld AI, 3 University of Copenhagen, 4 University of Montreal, 5 McGill University
SIGGRAPH Asia 2025
Paper arXiv Video

Built to satisfy incompressibility and slip-boundary conditions by construction, our neural kinematic bases enable real-time, interactive fluid editing and simulation that generalizes to new domains.

Abstract

We propose mesh-free fluid simulations that exploit a kinematic neural basis for velocity fields represented by an MLP. We design a set of losses that ensures that these neural bases approximate fundamental physical properties such as orthogonality, divergence-free, boundary alignment, and smoothness. Our neural bases can then be used to fit an input sketch of a flow, which will inherit the same fundamental properties from the bases. We then can animate such flow in real-time using standard time integrators. Our neural bases can accommodate different domains, moving boundaries, and naturally extend to three dimensions.

Video

Method Overview

We represent each kinematic basis as a continuous velocity field encoded by a small MLP. The network is trained without simulation data and learns directly from physical principles: it produces divergence-free motion, aligns naturally with solid boundaries, and remains smooth and consistent across different domains. During training, the network sees only simple geometric cues, allowing it to generalize to new shapes without retraining. Once learned, these bases can be combined in real time to create fluid motions that remain physically valid while responding instantly to user edits.

Results

Below are several video demonstrations showcasing our method can handle separate obstacles, moving boundaries, and changing domain topology, both in 2D and 3D.

Separate Obstalce Components

Our method handles separate obstacles.​ In this scene, both the duck and the rock act as solid boundaries, and the particles follow the flow while cleanly staying outside both surfaces.

Moving Boundary

Our method naturally handles moving boundaries.​ Here we place a fast fan and a slow fan, and release passive particles in the water to observe the flow.​ The fast fan pushes the particles outward quickly, while the slow fan creates only gentle motion.​

Changing Domain Topology

Our method adapts to dynamic topology changes. As the gripper opens and closes to reshape the domain, the flow responds instantly, pushing particles in and out of the closing gap.

3D Moving Boundary

We show a rotating fan in 3D.​ It generates a leading vortex in the front and a wake vortex behind, just as we expect from classical rotor hydrodynamics.

3D Fixed Boundary

The 3D model also generalizes across diverse domains in real time.​
